Tell Daniel It Wasn’t Meant for Me

A courtroom drama in dialogue

Son / Daniel

Murderer / Cross

The courtroom is too quiet. The ceiling fans hum. A bailiff closes the side door with a firm click. The judge settles into his chair. At the defense table, the lawyer leans in, one hand over a yellow legal pad. The murderer sits beside him in county blues, expression controlled but not calm. In the front row, the son of the murdered man stands when he should be sitting, fingers locked around the back of the bench in front of him.
JudgeMr. Vale, sit down.
SonI just want to look at him.
JudgeSit down now.
The son sits, but only barely, perched on the edge of the bench. His eyes stay fixed on the defendant.
LawyerYour Honor, before the jury is brought in, the defense renews its objection to the inclusion of the victim impact photographs. Their prejudicial effect is obvious.
JudgeI ruled on that yesterday.
LawyerAnd I am preserving the record today.
JudgeRecord preserved.
The lawyer nods once, then turns slightly toward his client.
LawyerDon't react to anything. Not to him, not to the photographs, not to the testimony. Look at me or look down.
MurdererI know how to sit in a chair.
LawyerThen do it like your life depends on it.
The murderer lets out a breath through his nose. Across the aisle, the son laughs once, sharp and humorless.
SonThat's the first true thing anybody's said in here.
JudgeMr. Vale.
SonHe's worried about how he sits? My father was found in a ditch.
LawyerMy client has the right to a fair trial.
SonYour client had my father begging on his knees.
Silence. Even the hum of the fans seems to recede.
MurdererThat's not what happened.
The son rises so fast the bench creaks.
SonYou don't get to tell me what happened.
JudgeSit down, or I will have you removed.
SonThen remove me.
JudgeMr. Vale—
SonNo, let him talk. Let him say it. He's been sitting there for four days pretending he's made of stone. Let him say what happened.
The lawyer stands now too, one palm pressed to the table.
LawyerMy client will not be baited into an outburst because the gallery cannot control itself.
SonThe gallery?
LawyerYou are not a party to this case.
SonHe killed my father.
LawyerAllegedly.
The son stares at the lawyer as if he may cross the aisle.
SonSay that again.
JudgeCounselor.
LawyerWithdrawn.
MurdererHe was alive when I left.
The son goes still. The judge's face hardens. The lawyer slowly turns to look at his client.
LawyerDon't say another word.
SonThere. There. You hear that? You all hear that?
JudgeThe statement will be stricken.
SonStricken?
LawyerYour Honor, my client is under enormous pressure—
SonHe just said it.
MurdererI said he was alive.
SonAnd you know that because you were there.
LawyerStop talking.
MurdererHe was breathing.
LawyerStop.
The murderer looks past everyone now, directly at the son.
MurdererYour father looked at me like he knew me.
The son's mouth opens, but nothing comes out at first.
SonWhat does that mean?
LawyerYour Honor, I need a recess.
JudgeYou needed control of your client five seconds ago.
SonWhat does that mean?
MurdererHe said a name.
SonMy father said your name?
MurdererNo.
SonThen whose?
The murderer's jaw tightens. For the first time, he looks uncertain.
LawyerDo not answer that.
JudgeBailiff, clear the courtroom except counsel, the defendant, and Mr. Vale.
SonNo. No, don't you dare send me out now.
JudgeMr. Vale, if there is even a chance this concerns a matter not yet in evidence, I will control how it enters this courtroom.
SonThat man put a bullet in my father and now we're protecting procedure?
JudgeWe are protecting the trial. Sit down and keep your voice level.
The bailiff hesitates at the back doors, waiting.
LawyerYour Honor, my client is not waiving anything. He is exhausted, he is frightened, and he is misspeaking.
MurdererI'm not frightened.
LawyerYou should be.
The judge folds his hands.
JudgeMr. Cross, I am going to ask you one question, and your attorney may advise you whether to answer.
The murderer says nothing.
JudgeDid the victim speak before his death?
The lawyer leans in, whispering hard and fast. The murderer listens, eyes lowered. The son stands motionless, staring.
LawyerDo not answer. Do you understand me? Do not answer.
The whispering stops.
JudgeMr. Cross?
A long beat.
MurdererYes.
The son takes one step forward into the aisle.
SonWhat did he say?
LawyerWe are done here.
JudgeWhat did he say, Mr. Cross?
The murderer looks at the son again, and when he speaks, his voice is quieter than before.
MurdererHe said, "Tell Daniel it wasn't meant for me."
The son goes pale.
JudgeDaniel?
SonThat's me.
No one moves.
LawyerYour Honor, recess. Now.
JudgeGranted.
The gavel comes down once. The crack echoes. But nobody immediately leaves.
— recess —
The gavel's echo lingers, but the room stays frozen. The bailiff looks to the judge for instruction and gets none. The son—Daniel—has one hand on the rail now, knuckles white. The lawyer is already gathering papers with shaking precision, as if neat stacks might restore control. The murderer sits very still.
DanielNo.
It comes out barely above a whisper.
DanielNo, he didn't.
JudgeMr. Vale—
DanielHe didn't call me Daniel.
The murderer's eyes narrow, studying him.
MurdererHe did.
DanielHe called me Danny.
The lawyer closes his eyes for half a second.
LawyerThat is exactly why no one should be speaking right now.
DanielHe called me Danny when he was happy, and "son" when he was angry, and "Daniel" only when—
He stops. His face changes.
JudgeOnly when what?
DanielOnly when he thought I was lying.
The judge watches him carefully.
JudgeSit down, Mr. Vale.
DanielNo.
LawyerDaniel—
DanielDon't say my name like you know me.
LawyerFine. Then listen to me, Mr. Vale. If there is some fact buried in what was just said, blurting it out in open court is the fastest way to poison it.
DanielPoison it for who?
LawyerFor the truth.
DanielYou don't care about the truth.
LawyerI care about what can be proven.
DanielSame thing to you?
LawyerNever.
That lands harder than anyone expects. Even Daniel pauses.
JudgeCounsel, in chambers. Now.
DanielNo. He says something like that and now everybody disappears behind a door?
JudgeMr. Vale, you are here because I allowed it. Do not mistake that for authority.
DanielThen tell me why my father would say that.
MurdererBecause he thought you were next.
The lawyer slams a palm on the table.
LawyerEnough!
JudgeMr. Cross, one more outburst and I will have your attorney gag your side of this table with procedure if I have to.
MurdererHe asked.
LawyerHe is grieving. He is not examining you.
DanielNext for what?
The murderer turns toward him fully now. His voice loses its earlier edge. It is flatter, more dangerous for how plain it is.
MurdererYour father owed someone. I was there to send a message.
DanielYou shot him.
MurdererI said I was there to send a message.
DanielThat is the message.
LawyerStop answering selectively as if that helps you.
JudgeMr. Cross, answer this: are you claiming someone else ordered the killing?
The lawyer points a finger at the bench.
LawyerHe invokes.
MurdererYes.
The lawyer's hand drops.
LawyerYou idiot.
JudgeName them.
LawyerDo not.
DanielName them.
The murderer looks at Daniel, not the judge.
MurdererYour father said you took something.
DanielI don't know what you're talking about.
MurdererThen he died for nothing.
Daniel lunges forward before he knows he's moving. The bailiff is between them in an instant, catching him across the chest. The bench screeches. Papers slide from the defense table.
BailiffBack! Back!
DanielYou lying bastard!
JudgeMr. Vale!
LawyerSit him down or get him out.
DanielHe's trying to bury this on me!
MurdererI'm telling you what he said.
DanielYou expect me to believe my father's last words were about me stealing something?
MurdererNot stealing.
DanielThen what?
The murderer doesn't answer right away. The silence feels deliberate now.
JudgeMr. Cross, if you are going to keep speaking, then finish a sentence for once in your life.
MurdererHe said you kept records.
Daniel's anger falters. That tiny hesitation is impossible to miss.
LawyerYour Honor—
JudgeI saw it.
DanielRecords of what?
MurdererPayments. Names. Dates.
DanielMy father sold auto parts.
MurdererYour father sold whatever men needed him to sell.
DanielNo.
MurdererYou really didn't know?
The lawyer studies Daniel now with a new, colder interest. The judge sees it too.
JudgeMr. Vale, is there anything you need to tell this court?
DanielNo.
JudgeThat answer came too fast.
DanielBecause there's nothing to tell.
MurdererHe said the ledger would keep you alive.
Daniel stops struggling against the bailiff.
DanielLedger?
MurdererThat's the word he used.
LawyerThis is worthless unless it's anchored to something real.
JudgeMany things are real before they are admissible, counselor.
LawyerAnd many lies become dangerous when spoken in the right tone.
JudgeTrue.
The judge rises. Everyone straightens instinctively.
JudgeHere is what will happen. Mr. Cross will say nothing further in this room outside the presence of a court reporter and, if necessary, under seal. Counsel will join me in chambers immediately. Mr. Vale—
Daniel looks up.
JudgeYou will remain seated and silent unless I call for you.
DanielAnd if I don't?
JudgeThen you spend the afternoon in a holding cell reflecting on the difference between pain and permission.
Daniel gives a short, bitter laugh.
DanielYou sound like my father.
The judge holds his gaze for a moment longer than expected.
JudgeThen your father had some discipline in him.
The murderer stands as the bailiff moves to reposition him. As he does, he leans just slightly toward Daniel.
MurdererCheck the green toolbox.
The lawyer wheels on him.
LawyerStop talking!
DanielWhat green toolbox?
But the bailiff is already turning the murderer toward the side door. The judge has gone still again.
JudgeWhat toolbox, Mr. Cross?
The murderer glances back over his shoulder. This time, when he smiles, it is the first honest expression he has shown.
MurdererAsk his mother.
The side door shuts behind him.
— the door shuts —
The side door shuts, and the sound seems to split the room in two. On one side: the judge, rigid behind the bench. On the other: Daniel, still half-held by the bailiff, breathing through his mouth like he's been running. The lawyer remains by the table, one hand flat on the scattered papers, staring at the door as if he could force the words back inside.
DanielMy mother?
No one answers immediately.
JudgeBailiff, release him.
BailiffYour Honor—
JudgeHe is not under arrest.
The bailiff lets go. Daniel doesn't move. He's looking at the empty doorway.
LawyerThat was intentional.
DanielWhat was?
LawyerThe toolbox. Your mother. The pause before each line. He's turning this into a maze because he has nothing solid to bargain with except confusion.
DanielYou think he made that up?
LawyerI think men like him don't waste words unless they buy something.
JudgeThat applies to more than men like him.
The lawyer glances up at the bench, accepts the hit, says nothing.
DanielMy mother's been dead six years.
That changes the air.
JudgeI'm sorry.
DanielDon't.
The judge's expression hardens again, less from offense than restraint.
LawyerDid your father keep old tools? Storage? Garage? A workshop?
DanielEverybody keeps tools.
LawyerGreen toolbox, Daniel.
DanielStop using my name like we're on the same side.
LawyerAt the moment, I'm on the side of finding out whether my client just injected a dead woman into a murder trial to distract from his own exposure.
DanielYour client shot my father.
LawyerVery possibly. And still, he may also be telling the truth about something else.
DanielYou say that like it's normal.
LawyerIt is normal. That's the part people hate.
Daniel turns away from him, up toward the bench.
DanielHe said "ask his mother" because he knew she was dead?
JudgeMaybe.
DanielHow would he know that?
JudgeMaybe because someone told him. Maybe because he knew your father longer than you think. Maybe because he's lying and dead people make useful witnesses.
DanielWhich one do you believe?
JudgeI don't believe. I sort.
The judge steps down from the bench now. It is not dramatic, but it is unusual enough that everyone notices.
JudgeCounsel, chambers can wait two minutes.
LawyerYour Honor—
JudgeTwo minutes.
The lawyer nods once.
JudgeMr. Vale. Look at me.
Daniel does.
JudgeWhen your father used "Daniel," what kind of lie did he think you were telling?
Daniel's eyes narrow. He knows this is not sympathy. It is precision.
DanielThe kind where I already knew the answer.
JudgeGood. So when Mr. Cross said your father used your formal name, what was your first thought?
DanielThat my father thought I was hiding something.
JudgeWere you?
DanielNo.
LawyerThat is not what your face said.
Daniel turns on him.
DanielMy face?
LawyerWhen he said "records," you flinched. When he said "ledger," you stopped breathing for a full second.
DanielYou don't know what you saw.
LawyerI make a living on less.
JudgeEnough. Mr. Vale, answer carefully. Had you ever heard the word "ledger" used in connection with your father before today?
Daniel says nothing.
JudgeThat is not a difficult question.
DanielOnce.
The lawyer straightens.
LawyerFrom whom?
DanielMy mother.
The courtroom goes quiet again.
JudgeWhen?
DanielYears ago. I was maybe fifteen.
LawyerIn what context?
DanielShe and my father were fighting in the kitchen. She said, "One day that ledger is going to bury us." He told her to lower her voice because I was upstairs.
JudgeAnd were you upstairs?
DanielI was on the stairs.
LawyerDid you ever ask him?
DanielHe said it was tax stuff.
LawyerDid you believe him?
DanielI was fifteen.
LawyerThat's not an answer.
DanielNo.
The judge folds his arms.
JudgeWhy not?
DanielBecause my mother was scared.
JudgeOf your father?
DanielNo.
LawyerOf who, then?
DanielOf the people my father was scared of.
The lawyer looks back toward the side door.
LawyerThen Mr. Cross may not have been freelancing.
DanielYou sound pleased.
LawyerI sound interested.
DanielSame thing to you?
LawyerOften.
The judge cuts in before Daniel can answer.
JudgeDid your mother ever mention a toolbox?
DanielNo.
JudgeDid your father own one that was distinct enough to be referred to that way?
DanielIn the garage, there was an old metal one. Green. Rust on the corners. He never let me touch it.
LawyerThere it is.
DanielThat proves nothing.
LawyerIt proves your client— excuse me, the victim— kept exactly the kind of object Mr. Cross named without prompting.
DanielOr he'd been to our house.
That lands.
JudgeHad he?
DanielI don't know.
LawyerDid your father know him?
DanielI never saw them together.
LawyerNot the question.
DanielThen I don't know.
The judge looks to the bailiff.
JudgeHas the jury been kept out?
BailiffYes, Your Honor.
JudgeGood.
He turns back.
JudgeMr. Vale, where is this toolbox now?
Daniel swallows.
DanielI don't know.
LawyerAnother bad answer.
DanielWe cleaned the garage after my father died.
Judge"We" meaning?
DanielMe and my uncle.
LawyerYour father's brother?
DanielMy mother's.
LawyerName.
DanielRay Donnelly.
The lawyer's eyes sharpen at once.
LawyerDonnelly.
JudgeYou know it?
LawyerI know the name.
DanielFrom where?
LawyerI'm not sure yet.
DanielThat's convenient.
LawyerIt's accurate.
JudgeSave it. Mr. Vale, did this uncle take anything from the garage?
Daniel hesitates.
JudgeMr. Vale.
DanielHe said he was taking paperwork so I wouldn't have to deal with it.
LawyerWhat paperwork?
DanielI didn't ask.
LawyerWhy not?
DanielBecause my father had just been murdered!
LawyerYes, and people tend to hide things inside grief because no one wants to press them while they're bleeding.
JudgeCounselor.
LawyerThat was not a metaphor I needed.
Daniel rubs his face hard with both hands.
DanielAre you saying my uncle knew?
LawyerI'm saying someone might have cleaned the board before anyone else saw the pieces.
DanielStop talking like it's a game.
LawyerThen start treating it like it isn't.
The judge's tone drops.
JudgeMr. Vale. Is there any reason— any reason at all— that your uncle would know to remove a ledger from your father's garage immediately after the killing?
Daniel looks from the judge to the lawyer, then back to the floor.
DanielHe got to the house before I did.
JudgeWho called him?
DanielI didn't.
LawyerWho told you he took paperwork?
DanielHe did. Later. He said, "I cleaned up some of your dad's mess."
JudgeExact words?
Daniel"Some of your dad's mess." Yes.
The lawyer lets out a slow breath.
LawyerYour Honor, chambers is now urgent.
JudgeI'm aware.
DanielWhat happens now?
JudgeNow I find out whether your uncle Ray Donnelly is a grieving relative, a loose end, or the reason a defendant just blew up his own defense in open court.
DanielAnd me?
JudgeThat depends.
DanielOn what?
The judge studies him carefully.
JudgeOn whether your father died trying to protect you, or because of you.
Daniel recoils like he's been struck.
DanielI didn't do anything.
LawyerThen this is the moment to remember what you did do.
A knock sounds at the side door. The bailiff opens it a crack. A clerk leans in, pale, rattled.
ClerkYour Honor— sorry— there's a call for Mr. Vale. They said it couldn't wait.
JudgeFrom whom?
The clerk looks down at the note in her hand.
ClerkThey only said, "Tell Daniel to stop opening old boxes."
Daniel goes white.
LawyerDo not take that call alone.
JudgePut it on speaker in chambers.
DanielNo.
All three turn to him.
DanielIf this is about my father, they want me scared. I'm not giving them a room and an audience.
LawyerThis is not a performance choice.
DanielIt's my call.
JudgeIn my courthouse, it is not.
The phone begins ringing again in the clerk's trembling hand.
“Tell Daniel it wasn’t meant for me.”
